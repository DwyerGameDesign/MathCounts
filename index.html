Core Formulas
Permutation — Order Matters
P(n,r) = n! / (n−r)!
Arrange r items from n distinct items. Think: passwords, race placements, seating in a row.
Example
Problem: How many ways can 5 runners finish in 1st, 2nd, and 3rd place?
P(n,r) = n! / (n−r)!
↓
P(5,3) = 5! / (5−3)!
Calculate: = 5! / 2! = 120 / 2 = 60 ways
Combination — Order Doesn't Matter
C(n,r) = n! / (r!(n−r)!)
Choose r items from n. Think: committees, hands of cards, pizza toppings.
Example
Problem: A committee of 3 is chosen from 10 people. How many committees are possible?
C(n,r) = n! / (r!(n−r)!)
↓
C(10,3) = 10! / (3!(10−3)!)
= 10! / (3!·7!)
Calculate: = 3,628,800 / (6·5,040) = 3,628,800 / 30,240 = 120 committees
Permutations with Repetition
n! / (a! · b! · c! …)
Arrange items where some are identical. Ex: letters in "BANANA" = 6!/(3!·2!·1!)
Example
Problem: How many ways can you arrange the letters in "MAMA"?
n! / (a! · b! · c! …)
↓
4! / (2! · 2!)
M appears 2 times, A appears 2 times
Calculate: = 24 / (2 · 2) = 24 / 4 = 6 arrangements
Circular Permutations
(n−1)!
Arranging n items around a circle. One item is "fixed" to remove rotational duplicates. Necklaces = (n−1)!/2.
Example
Problem: How many ways can 6 people sit around a round table?
(n−1)!
↓
(6−1)! = 5!
Calculate: = 5! = 120 = 120 arrangements
For a necklace (flipable): (6−1)!/2 = 120/2 = 60 arrangements
Stars & Bars (Distributions)
C(n+k−1, k−1)
Distribute n identical items into k distinct bins (zeros allowed). Ex: cookies into jars.
Example
Problem: Distribute 10 identical cookies among 4 children (some may get 0).
C(n+k−1, k−1)
↓
C(10+4−1, 4−1) = C(13, 3)
n = 10 cookies, k = 4 children
Calculate: = 13! / (3!·10!) = 286 ways
If each must get ≥1: Give each 1 cookie first, then distribute remaining 6:
C(6+4−1, 4−1) = C(9, 3) = 84 ways
Derangements (Nothing in Place)
D(n) = n!·Σ(−1)ᵏ/k!
Permutations where no item appears in its original position. D(n) ≈ n!/e. D(1)=0, D(2)=1, D(3)=2, D(4)=9.
Example
Problem: In how many ways can 4 people each get the wrong hat?
D(n) = n! − (arrangements with ≥1 fixed)
↓
D(4) = 4! − C(4,1)·3! + C(4,2)·2! − C(4,3)·1! + C(4,4)·0!
Using PIE: Total − (at least 1 correct)
Calculate: = 24 − 4·6 + 6·2 − 4·1 + 1·1 = 24 − 24 + 12 − 4 + 1 = 9 ways
How to Approach Problems

The Decision Framework

1
Does order matter? If swapping two items gives a different outcome → Permutation. If not → Combination. Ask: "Is ABC the same as BAC?"
2
Are items identical or distinct? If some items repeat (letters, balls of same color), use multinomial. If all distinct, standard P/C.
3
Are there restrictions? Handle restrictions first — fix constrained items, then count the rest freely.
4
Is it a distribution problem? Identical items into bins → Stars & Bars. Distinct items into bins → exponential counting (kⁿ).
5
Check if complementary counting is easier: If the restriction is "at least one" or "not all," often it's faster to count the opposite.
Gotchas — Click to Expand
⚠️ Circular vs. Linear Seating +
In a line: n! arrangements. In a circle: (n−1)! arrangements because rotations are the same. Necklaces also remove reflections: (n−1)!/2. Don't forget which one the problem is asking!
⚠️ "At Least" Restrictions +
Problems saying "at least one woman" are almost always easier with complementary: Total − (none of the restricted type). Don't do painful casework when you don't have to.
⚠️ Treating Identical Items as Distinct +
If two red balls are considered identical, you must divide by the factorial of duplicates. Forgetting this is the #1 overcounting mistake. Always ask: "Are these items actually distinguishable?"
⚠️ People "Together" Trick +
When a group must stay together, glue them into one "super-item." Count arrangements of (remaining + 1 super-item), then multiply by arrangements within the group. Don't forget the internal arrangements!
⚠️ Stars & Bars Pitfalls +
Stars & Bars works for non-negative integers only. If each bin must have at least 1, subtract 1 from each bin first: give everyone 1, then distribute the remainder freely. New formula: C((n−k)+(k−1), k−1).
⚠️ Choosing vs. Arranging Sub-Groups +
Selecting a committee of 3 from 10 is C(10,3). Selecting and then ranking them (president, VP, secretary) is P(10,3). The difference is the extra 3! factor for internal ordering.
Practice Problems
EX 01 In how many ways can 8 people be seated at a round table if two specific people must NOT sit next to each other? ▶
Solution
Use complementary counting. Total circular arrangements = (8−1)! = 5040.

Bad arrangements (the two sit together): Glue them as a unit → 7 items in a circle = (7−1)! = 720. The pair can swap internally in 2! = 2 ways. So bad = 720 × 2 = 1440.

Answer = 5040 − 1440 = 3600 3600
EX 02 How many ways can the letters of "MISSISSIPPI" be arranged? ▶
Solution
Count letter frequencies: M=1, I=4, S=4, P=2. Total = 11 letters.

Apply multinomial formula: 11! / (1! · 4! · 4! · 2!)

= 39,916,800 / (1 · 24 · 24 · 2) = 39,916,800 / 1152 34,650
EX 03 In how many ways can 10 identical candies be distributed among 4 children if each child must receive at least 1? ▶
Solution
Since each child needs ≥1, give each child 1 candy first (uses 4). We now distribute 6 remaining among 4 children with no restriction.

Stars & Bars: C(6 + 4 − 1, 4 − 1) = C(9, 3) 84
Core Formulas
Basic Probability
P(A) = favourable / total
All outcomes must be equally likely! If they aren't, you can't just count — you need weighted probabilities.
Example
Problem: What's the probability of rolling an even number on a fair die?
P(A) = favourable / total
↓
P(even) = 3 / 6
Favourable: {2, 4, 6} = 3 | Total: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} = 6
Calculate: = 1/2
Complement Rule
P(A) = 1 − P(Aᶜ)
The probability something happens = 1 minus the probability it doesn't. Essential for "at least one" problems.
Example
Problem: What's the probability of rolling at least one 6 in 3 dice rolls?
P(A) = 1 − P(Aᶜ)
↓
P(at least one 6) = 1 − P(no 6s)
Calculate P(no 6s): P(no 6 on one die) = 5/6
P(no 6s in 3 rolls) = (5/6)³ = 125/216
Substitute: P(at least one) = 1 − 125/216
Calculate: = 216/216 − 125/216 = 91/216
Multiplication Rule
P(A∩B) = P(A)·P(B|A)
For dependent events. If independent: P(A∩B) = P(A)·P(B). "AND" = multiply.
Example
Problem: Draw 2 cards without replacement. What's P(both are aces)?
P(A∩B) = P(A)·P(B|A)
↓
P(both aces) = P(1st ace) · P(2nd ace | 1st ace)
Step 1: P(1st ace) = 4/52 = 1/13
Step 2: P(2nd ace | 1st was ace) = 3/51 = 1/17
Substitute: P(both aces) = (1/13) · (1/17)
Calculate: = 1/221
Addition Rule
P(A∪B) = P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B)
For any two events. If mutually exclusive (no overlap): just P(A) + P(B). "OR" = add, then subtract overlap.
Example
Problem: Roll a die. What's P(prime OR even)?
P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A∩B)
↓
P(prime ∪ even) = P(prime) + P(even) − P(prime ∩ even)
= 3/6 + 3/6 − 1/6
Primes: {2, 3, 5} | Even: {2, 4, 6} | Both: {2}
Calculate: = 5/6
Expected Value
E = Σ xᵢ · P(xᵢ)
Weighted average of all outcomes. Multiply each outcome by its probability and sum them up. Common in games/dice problems.
Example
Problem: A game pays $10 for rolling 6, $5 for rolling 3–5, $0 otherwise. What's the expected value?
E = Σ xᵢ · P(xᵢ)
↓
E = $10·(1/6) + $5·(3/6) + $0·(2/6)
Roll 6: $10, P=1/6 | Roll 3–5: $5, P=3/6 | Roll 1–2: $0, P=2/6
Calculate: = 10/6 + 15/6 + 0 = 25/6 = $4.17
Geometric Probability
P = favourable area / total area
When the sample space is a region. Set up a ratio of areas, lengths, or volumes. Watch for symmetry!
Example
Problem: A point is chosen randomly in a 2×2 square. What's P(it's within 1 unit of a corner)?
P = favourable area / total area
↓
P = π / 4
Total: 2×2 = 4 | Favourable: 4 quarter-circles (r=1) = π
Calculate: ≈ 0.785
How to Approach Problems

Probability Problem Framework

1
Identify the sample space: What are all possible outcomes? Are they equally likely? (Dice rolls: yes. Random points: use area.)
2
Are events independent or dependent? Drawing without replacement = dependent. Flipping separate coins = independent. This determines if you multiply or use conditional probability.
3
"At least one" → use complement: P(at least one) = 1 − P(none). Saves enormous casework.
4
Multi-stage processes: Draw a tree or use multiplication for sequential events. Track how the denominator shrinks (without replacement).
5
Simplify the fraction: MATHCOUNTS often wants a simplified fraction or a decimal. Always double-check your answer is ≤ 1!
Gotchas — Click to Expand
⚠️ With vs. Without Replacement +
Without replacement: denominator shrinks each draw. If you draw 2 from 10, first draw is /10, second is /9. With replacement: denominator stays the same. The problem will usually specify — if it doesn't, assume WITHOUT replacement for physical objects like cards, marbles.
⚠️ Equally Likely Outcomes +
You can only use "favorable/total" if ALL outcomes are equally likely. A loaded die, a spinner with unequal sectors, or two different coins — you can't just count outcomes, you must weight by probability.
⚠️ Confusing AND / OR +
"A and B" = intersection = multiply probabilities. "A or B" = union = add then subtract overlap. Many students add when they should multiply and vice versa. Keyword scan: look for "and/both" vs. "or/either."
⚠️ Order Matters in Probability +
When computing P(exactly 2 heads in 3 flips), don't forget to count HHT, HTH, THH as separate outcomes. If you use combinations in numerator, use combinations in denominator too — stay consistent. Mixing ordered/unordered breaks everything.
⚠️ The "First Card" Trap +
When finding P(2 aces drawn from 52 cards), you can use C(4,2)/C(52,2) = 6/1326. Don't compute (4/52)×(3/51) = 1/221 thinking these are different — they equal the same thing! Both methods work but you must be consistent.
⚠️ Expected Value ≠ Most Likely +
Expected value is the long-run average, not the most common outcome. E(rolling a die) = 3.5, which you can never actually roll. Don't confuse EV with the mode or median of outcomes.
Practice Problems
EX 01 A bag has 5 red and 4 blue marbles. Two marbles are drawn without replacement. What is the probability both are red? ▶
Solution
Method 1 (Sequential): P(red) × P(red | first was red) = (5/9) × (4/8) = 20/72 = 5/18

Method 2 (Combinations): C(5,2)/C(9,2) = 10/36 = 5/18 ✓ 5/18
EX 02 What is the probability of rolling at least one 6 when rolling two dice? ▶
Solution
Use the complement: P(at least one 6) = 1 − P(no 6s).

P(no 6 on one die) = 5/6. Two independent dice:
P(no 6s) = (5/6)² = 25/36

P(at least one 6) = 1 − 25/36 11/36
EX 03 A game pays $5 if you flip 3 heads with 3 coins, $2 for exactly 2 heads, and nothing otherwise. What is the expected value? ▶
Solution
P(3 heads) = (1/2)³ = 1/8. Payout: $5
P(2 heads) = C(3,2)·(1/8) = 3/8. Payout: $2
P(else) = 4/8. Payout: $0

E = 5·(1/8) + 2·(3/8) + 0·(4/8) = 5/8 + 6/8 $1.375 (= 11/8)
The Core Idea
Complementary Counting
Desired = Total − Unwanted
Count everything, then subtract what you don't want. Use when the restriction is complex but the opposite is simple.
Example
Problem: How many 4-digit numbers have at least one 7?
Desired = Total − Unwanted
↓
Numbers with at least one 7 = Total − Numbers with NO 7
= 9,000 − 5,832
Total: 9×10×10×10 = 9,000 | Unwanted: 8×9×9×9 = 5,832
Calculate: = 3,168
Inclusion-Exclusion (PIE)
|A∪B| = |A|+|B|−|A∩B|
For 3 sets: |A∪B∪C| = |A|+|B|+|C| − |A∩B| − |A∩C| − |B∩C| + |A∩B∩C|
Example
Problem: How many integers 1–100 are divisible by 3 or 5?
|A∪B| = |A| + |B| − |A∩B|
↓
|divisible by 3 or 5| = |divisible by 3| + |divisible by 5| − |divisible by 15|
= 33 + 20 − 6
|A| = ⌊100/3⌋ = 33 | |B| = ⌊100/5⌋ = 20 | |A∩B| = ⌊100/15⌋ = 6
Calculate: = 47
Derangements (Complement)
D(n) = n! − (arrangements with ≥1 fixed)
Use PIE to subtract arrangements where at least one item is in its original position. Shortcut: D(n) ≈ round(n!/e)
Example
Problem: 5 people each get a wrong hat. How many ways?
D(n) = n! − (arrangements with ≥1 fixed)
↓
D(5) = 5! − C(5,1)·4! + C(5,2)·3! − C(5,3)·2! + C(5,4)·1! − C(5,5)·0!
Using PIE: Total − (at least 1 correct)
Calculate: = 120 − 5·24 + 10·6 − 10·2 + 5·1 − 1·1
= 120 − 120 + 60 − 20 + 5 − 1 = 44 ways
When to Use Complementary Counting

Trigger Words & Patterns

1
"At least one": Almost always easier to do 1 − P(none). Counting "at least one of X" via casework means summing exactly 1, exactly 2, etc. — much slower.
2
"Not all the same" / "Not all different": The complement (all same, or all different) is usually much simpler to count directly.
3
Many cases, simple opposite: If direct counting requires 5+ cases but the complement is 1 calculation, go complementary.
4
PIE trigger — "divisible by A OR B": Count divisible by A, add divisible by B, subtract divisible by both (LCM). Classic number theory + counting hybrid.
5
Restriction avoidance: "No two adjacent" or "none in a row" often yields 2–3 casework situations directly, but complement (some are adjacent) is fewer and cleaner.
Gotchas — Click to Expand
⚠️ Forgetting to Account for All Cases +
When you subtract the unwanted, make sure your "unwanted" set is exactly what you don't want — not a subset. If you want "not all red" and you only subtract "all red," you're correct. But if the problem is more complex, a Venn diagram helps ensure you've covered every case.
⚠️ Double-Counting in PIE +
In PIE, things in multiple sets get added multiple times. The alternating +/− pattern fixes this. For 2 sets: add both, subtract overlap. For 3 sets: add all three, subtract pairwise overlaps, add back the triple overlap. Mess up the sign and you'll over- or undercount.
⚠️ Total Must Match Your Space +
Your "Total" must use the same rules as your desired count. If you're counting ordered pairs, the total must be ordered pairs. If you're counting subsets, total must be subsets. Mixing ordered and unordered totals is a very sneaky mistake.
⚠️ Complementary with Probability +
In probability, complementary is P(A) = 1 − P(Aᶜ). In counting, it's |A| = Total − |Aᶜ|. These are the same idea but apply differently. Make sure you know if you're computing a count or a probability — the setup differs.
⚠️ PIE Denominator Stays Fixed +
When using PIE for probability, the denominator of all fractions should be the same total sample space. Don't reduce individual fractions before adding/subtracting — you'll lose the common denominator and create arithmetic errors.
Practice Problems
EX 01 How many integers from 1 to 100 are divisible by 3 OR by 5? ▶
Solution — PIE
Divisible by 3: ⌊100/3⌋ = 33
Divisible by 5: ⌊100/5⌋ = 20
Divisible by both (15): ⌊100/15⌋ = 6

PIE: 33 + 20 − 6 47
EX 02 How many 4-letter strings (A–Z) have at least one repeated letter? ▶
Solution — Complementary
Total 4-letter strings = 26⁴ = 456,976

All distinct letters = 26 × 25 × 24 × 23 = 358,800

At least one repeat = 456,976 − 358,800 98,176
EX 03 In a class of 30 students, 18 play soccer, 15 play basketball, and 7 play both. How many play neither? ▶
Solution — PIE + Complement
Students playing soccer OR basketball:
|S ∪ B| = 18 + 15 − 7 = 26

Students playing neither = Total − |S ∪ B|
= 30 − 26 4 students
Core Formulas
GCD (Greatest Common Divisor)
gcd(a,b) = gcd(b, a mod b)
Use Euclidean algorithm. For prime factorization: take minimum exponents. gcd(12,18) = gcd(2²·3, 2·3²) = 2·3 = 6
Example
Problem: Find gcd(48, 180)
gcd(a,b) = gcd(b, a mod b)
↓
gcd(48, 180) = gcd(180, 48)
= gcd(48, 180 mod 48) = gcd(48, 36)
= gcd(36, 48 mod 36) = gcd(36, 12)
= gcd(12, 36 mod 12) = gcd(12, 0) = 12
Or by prime factorization: 48 = 2⁴·3, 180 = 2²·3²·5
Take minimum exponents: 2²·3¹ = 12
LCM (Least Common Multiple)
lcm(a,b) = a·b / gcd(a,b)
For prime factorization: take maximum exponents. Always: gcd(a,b) · lcm(a,b) = a·b
Example
Problem: Find lcm(12, 18)
lcm(a,b) = a·b / gcd(a,b)
↓
lcm(12, 18) = 12·18 / gcd(12, 18)
= 216 / 6
Calculate: = 36

Or by prime factorization: 12 = 2²·3, 18 = 2·3²
Take maximum exponents: 2²·3² = 36
Number of Divisors
d(n) = (e₁+1)(e₂+1)…(eₖ+1)
If n = p₁ᵉ¹·p₂ᵉ²·…·pₖᵉᵏ, then d(n) counts all divisors. d(12) = d(2²·3¹) = (2+1)(1+1) = 6
Example
Problem: How many positive divisors does 360 have?
d(n) = (e₁+1)(e₂+1)…(eₖ+1)
↓
360 = 2³·3²·5¹
d(360) = (3+1)(2+1)(1+1)
Calculate: = 4·3·2 = 24 divisors
Sum of Divisors
σ(n) = (p₁ᵉ¹⁺¹−1)/(p₁−1) · (p₂ᵉ²⁺¹−1)/(p₂−1) · …
Sum of all positive divisors. For prime p: σ(pᵉ) = (pᵉ⁺¹−1)/(p−1). σ(12) = σ(2²·3¹) = (2³−1)/(2−1) · (3²−1)/(3−1) = 7·4 = 28
Example
Problem: Find the sum of all positive divisors of 18
σ(n) = (p₁ᵉ¹⁺¹−1)/(p₁−1) · (p₂ᵉ²⁺¹−1)/(p₂−1)
↓
18 = 2¹·3²
σ(18) = (2²−1)/(2−1) · (3³−1)/(3−1)
= 3/1 · 26/2 = 3 · 13
Calculate: = 39

Verify: Divisors: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18 → Sum = 39 ✓
Modular Arithmetic
(a+b) mod m = ((a mod m) + (b mod m)) mod m
Same for subtraction and multiplication. Division requires modular inverse. Useful for finding remainders of large numbers.
Example
Problem: What is 1234 + 5678 mod 7?
(a+b) mod m = ((a mod m) + (b mod m)) mod m
↓
(1234 + 5678) mod 7 = ((1234 mod 7) + (5678 mod 7)) mod 7
= (2 + 1) mod 7
Calculate: 1234 ÷ 7 = 176 R2, 5678 ÷ 7 = 811 R1
(2 + 1) mod 7 = 3
Fermat's Little Theorem
If p is prime, aᵖ⁻¹ ≡ 1 (mod p)
For gcd(a,p) = 1. Useful for finding large powers mod p. 2⁶ mod 7 = 2⁷⁻¹ mod 7 = 1 mod 7
Example
Problem: Find 3¹⁰⁰ mod 7
aᵖ⁻¹ ≡ 1 (mod p) if p is prime
↓
3⁶ ≡ 1 (mod 7) since 7 is prime
3¹⁰⁰ = 3⁶·¹⁶ + ⁴ = (3⁶)¹⁶ · 3⁴
≡ 1¹⁶ · 3⁴ (mod 7) = 3⁴ (mod 7)
Calculate: 3⁴ = 81 ≡ 4 (mod 7)
Answer: 4
Euler's Totient Function
φ(n) = n · Π(1 − 1/pᵢ)
Counts integers ≤ n coprime to n. For prime p: φ(p) = p−1. For pᵉ: φ(pᵉ) = pᵉ − pᵉ⁻¹
Example
Problem: Find φ(12)
φ(n) = n · Π(1 − 1/pᵢ)
↓
12 = 2²·3¹
φ(12) = 12 · (1−1/2) · (1−1/3)
= 12 · 1/2 · 2/3
Calculate: = 12 · 1/3 = 4

Verify: Coprime to 12: {1, 5, 7, 11} → 4 numbers ✓
Chinese Remainder Theorem
x ≡ a (mod m), x ≡ b (mod n)
If gcd(m,n) = 1, solution exists mod (m·n). Find x₁, x₂ such that m·x₁ + n·x₂ = 1, then x = a·n·x₂ + b·m·x₁
Example
Problem: Find x such that x ≡ 2 (mod 3) and x ≡ 3 (mod 5)
x ≡ a (mod m), x ≡ b (mod n)
↓
x ≡ 2 (mod 3), x ≡ 3 (mod 5)
Try x = 3k + 2, need 3k + 2 ≡ 3 (mod 5)
3k ≡ 1 (mod 5) → k ≡ 2 (mod 5)
k = 5m + 2, so x = 3(5m+2) + 2 = 15m + 8
Answer: x ≡ 8 (mod 15)
Verify: 8 mod 3 = 2 ✓, 8 mod 5 = 3 ✓
Divisibility Rules
2
Divisible by 2: Last digit is even (0, 2, 4, 6, 8)
3
Divisible by 3: Sum of digits is divisible by 3
4
Divisible by 4: Last 2 digits form a number divisible by 4
5
Divisible by 5: Last digit is 0 or 5
6
Divisible by 6: Divisible by both 2 and 3
7
Divisible by 7: Double last digit, subtract from rest. Result divisible by 7?
8
Divisible by 8: Last 3 digits form a number divisible by 8
9
Divisible by 9: Sum of digits is divisible by 9
11
Divisible by 11: Alternating sum of digits is divisible by 11
Practice Problems
EX 01 Find the smallest positive integer that leaves remainder 2 when divided by 3, remainder 3 when divided by 5, and remainder 4 when divided by 7. ▶
Solution — Chinese Remainder Theorem
We need x such that:
x ≡ 2 (mod 3), x ≡ 3 (mod 5), x ≡ 4 (mod 7)

Start with x ≡ 2 (mod 3) → x = 3k + 2
Substitute into second: 3k + 2 ≡ 3 (mod 5) → 3k ≡ 1 (mod 5) → k ≡ 2 (mod 5)
So k = 5m + 2, giving x = 3(5m + 2) + 2 = 15m + 8

Now x = 15m + 8 ≡ 4 (mod 7)
15m + 8 ≡ 4 (mod 7) → m + 1 ≡ 4 (mod 7) → m ≡ 3 (mod 7)
So m = 7n + 3, giving x = 15(7n + 3) + 8 = 105n + 53

Smallest positive: n = 0 → x = 53 53
EX 02 How many positive divisors does 360 have, and what is their sum? ▶
Solution
Prime factorization: 360 = 2³ · 3² · 5¹

Number of divisors: d(360) = (3+1)(2+1)(1+1) = 4·3·2 = 24 divisors

Sum of divisors: σ(360) = (2⁴−1)/(2−1) · (3³−1)/(3−1) · (5²−1)/(5−1)
= 15/1 · 26/2 · 24/4 = 15 · 13 · 6 = 1170 24 divisors, sum = 1170
EX 03 Find the last two digits of 7¹⁰⁰ ▶
Solution — Modular Arithmetic
We need 7¹⁰⁰ mod 100. Use Euler's theorem: φ(100) = φ(2²·5²) = 100·(1−1/2)(1−1/5) = 100·1/2·4/5 = 40

Since gcd(7, 100) = 1, 7⁴⁰ ≡ 1 (mod 100)
7¹⁰⁰ = 7⁴⁰·² + ²⁰ = (7⁴⁰)² · 7²⁰ ≡ 1² · 7²⁰ (mod 100)

Calculate 7²⁰ mod 100: 7² = 49, 7⁴ = 49² = 2401 ≡ 1 (mod 100)
So 7²⁰ = (7⁴)⁵ ≡ 1⁵ = 1 (mod 100)

Answer: 01 01
Lines, Angles & Triangles
Parallel Lines & Transversals
Alternate interior ∠s equal; same-side interior supplementary
When two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, use corresponding angles, alternate interior, and vertical angles to chase unknown measures.
Example
Problem: Two parallel lines meet a transversal. One acute angle is 38°. What is the obtuse angle on the same side of the transversal?
Same-side interior angles are supplementary (sum to 180°)
↓
180° − 38° = 142°
Answer: 142° (obtuse angle on that side of the transversal)
Triangle Angle Sum & Exterior Angle
∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180° · Ext. ∠ = sum of remote int. ∠s
An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles. Triangle inequality: any side < sum of the other two.
Example
Problem: In △ABC, exterior angle at C is 110° and ∠A = 40°. Find ∠B.
Ext. ∠ = ∠A + ∠B (remote interiors)
↓
110° = 40° + ∠B → ∠B = 70°
Answer: 70°
Congruence & Similarity
Congruent: SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS · Similar: AA (+ proportional sides)
Similar figures have equal corresponding angles; corresponding sides are in the same ratio k (scale factor).
Example
Problem: Two similar triangles have side ratio 2 : 5 (small to large). If the small triangle’s area is 12, what is the large triangle’s area?
Area ratio = k² where k is the linear scale factor (large/small)
↓
k = 5/2 → Area_large = (5/2)² · 12 = (25/4)·12
Calculate: = 25 · 3 = 75
Right Triangles & Circles
Pythagorean Theorem
a² + b² = c² (c hypotenuse)
Common triples: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17, 7-24-25 and any multiples. Converse: if a² + b² = c², the triangle is right.
Example
Problem: A right triangle has legs 9 and 12. Find the hypotenuse.
c² = 9² + 12² = 81 + 144 = 225
↓
c = 15
(3-4-5 scaled by 3)
Answer: 15
Special Right Triangles
45°-45°-90: 1 : 1 : √2 · 30°-60°-90: 1 : √3 : 2
In 30-60-90, the side opposite 30° is half the hypotenuse; opposite 60° is (√3/2) times hypotenuse.
Example
Problem: Hypotenuse of a 30-60-90 triangle is 10. Find the shorter leg.
Shorter leg opposite 30° = half hypotenuse
↓
10 / 2 = 5
Answer: 5
Circle Basics
C = 2πr = πd · A = πr²
Arc length = (θ/360°)·2πr (θ in degrees). Sector area = (θ/360°)·πr². Inscribed angle = ½ central angle subtending the same arc.
Example
Problem: Radius 6, central angle 60°. Find arc length and sector area.
Arc = (60/360)·2π(6) = (1/6)·12π
Sector = (60/360)·π(6)² = (1/6)·36π
Arc: 2π · Sector: 6π
Tangent & Secant (Power of a Point)
(Tangent)² = (external part) × (whole secant)
Tangent ⊥ radius at the point of tangency. Two tangents from an external point are equal. Inscribed angle = ½ central angle on the same arc.
Example
Problem: From an external point, a tangent to the circle has length 8. A secant through the same point has external segment 4 and meets the circle again so the total secant length is 4 + x (internal part x). Find x.
8² = 4 · (4 + x)
↓
64 = 16 + 4x → 4x = 48
Answer: x = 12
Area & Polygons
Triangle Area
A = ½bh = ½ab sin C · Heron: A = √(s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c))
s = semiperimeter = (a+b+c)/2. For a right triangle, A = ½(product of legs).
Example
Problem: Sides 5, 5, 6. Find area (isosceles).
Altitude to base 6 splits into 3+3; height h: 3² + h² = 5² → h = 4
↓
A = ½ · 6 · 4
Answer: 12
Quadrilaterals
□ A = bh · ▱ A = bh · Trapezoid A = ½(h)(b₁+b₂)
Rhombus: A = ½d₁d₂ (diagonals). Kite: same. Parallelogram: opposite sides parallel and equal.
Example
Problem: Trapezoid with height 5 and parallel bases 4 and 10. Find area.
A = ½(5)(4+10) = ½ · 5 · 14
Answer: 35
Regular Polygons
Interior sum = (n−2)·180° · Ext. ∠ = 360°/n
Each interior angle of a regular n-gon: (n−2)·180°/n. Number of diagonals: n(n−3)/2.
Example
Problem: One exterior angle of a regular polygon is 18°. How many sides?
n = 360° / (exterior angle)
↓
n = 360/18 = 20
Answer: 20 sides
Coordinate Geometry & 3D
Distance & Midpoint
d = √((x₂−x₁)²+(y₂−y₁)²) · M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)
Slope m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁). Parallel lines: equal slopes. Perpendicular: slopes multiply to −1 (except vertical/horizontal).
Example
Problem: Distance from (1,2) to (4,6)?
d² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25
↓
d = 5
Answer: 5
Prism & Cylinder
V_prism = B·h · V_cylinder = πr²h
B = base area. Lateral surface of cylinder = 2πrh; total = 2πrh + 2πr².
Example
Problem: Cylinder r = 3, h = 7. Volume?
V = π(3)²(7) = 63π
Answer: 63π cubic units
Pyramid, Cone & Sphere
V_pyramid = ⅓Bh · V_cone = ⅓πr²h · V_sphere = ⁴⁄₃πr³
Surface area: sphere A = 4πr²; cone lateral = πrℓ (ℓ slant height). Frustum: use similar triangles or subtract cones.
Example
Problem: Cone radius 4, height 3. Find volume.
V = ⅓π(4)²(3) = ⅓π·16·3 = 16π
Answer: 16π
Similar Solids
Linear k · Area k² · Volume k³
If all lengths scale by a factor k, surface areas scale by k² and volumes by k³.
Example
Problem: Two similar figures have lengths in ratio 2 : 3. What is the ratio of their volumes?
Volume ratio = k³ = (3/2)³ if comparing large to small
↓
8 : 27 (small to large)
Answer: 8 : 27
How to Approach Geometry Problems

Geometry Problem Framework

1
Draw a clean diagram and label knowns. Mark equal angles/sides from parallel lines, isosceles triangles, or congruent figures.
2
Identify the shape family: right triangle → Pythagorean or special triangles; circle → radii, arcs, inscribed angles; 3D → identify base and height.
3
Similarity: match corresponding vertices; set up proportional sides. Remember: area × k², volume × k³.
4
Coordinate problems: use distance, midpoint, slope; or place a convenient origin (e.g. vertex at (0,0)) to simplify algebra.
5
Sanity-check units: length vs area vs volume; angles in triangles must sum to 180°; probability-style “random point in region” uses area ratios.
Gotchas — Click to Expand
⚠️ Confusing Arc vs Full Circle +
Arc length and sector area use the fraction θ/360° (or θ/2π in radians). Don’t multiply by 2πr twice when you already used the fraction.
⚠️ Similarity: Area vs Length +
If sides double, area quadruples and volume octuples. A common error is using k instead of k² for area.
⚠️ Which Leg in 30-60-90? +
The side opposite 30° is the shortest (half the hypotenuse). Opposite 60° is √3 times the shortest leg. Sketch the triangle to avoid mixing them up.
⚠️ Inscribed vs Central Angle +
Inscribed angle = half the central angle for the same arc. An angle inscribed in a semicircle is 90°.
⚠️ Pyramid vs Prism Volume +
Prism: V = Bh. Pyramid/Cone: V = ⅓Bh. Losing the ⅓ factor is a classic mistake.
⚠️ Triangle Area: ½ ab sin C +
Use SAS (two sides and included angle) when you don’t have a convenient height. Don’t use sin of the wrong angle — it must be the included angle between the two sides.

Core Formulas
Distance, Rate & Time
d = r · t · r = d/t · t = d/r
Same units: distance per time. Meeting problems: closing speed = sum of speeds (toward each other). Chase: relative speed = difference.
Example
Problem: Two cars 210 mi apart drive toward each other at 45 mph and 60 mph. When do they meet?
Closing speed = 45 + 60 = 105 mph
↓
t = d / r = 210 / 105
Calculate: t = 2 hours
Ratios & Scaling
a : b = ka : kb · part / whole
Ratios stay equal when both parts multiply by the same factor. Splitting: ratio a:b → first gets a/(a+b) of the total.
Example
Problem: Divide $120 in the ratio 2 : 3. How much is the larger share?
Total parts = 2 + 3 = 5
↓
Larger share = (3/5) × 120
Calculate: = $72
Percents — Part & Whole
part = (p/100) · whole · whole = part / (p/100)
“What is p% of W?” → multiply. “p% of what is P?” → divide. Percent means “per 100.”
Example
Problem: 15% of a number is 42. What is the number?
0.15 × n = 42 → n = 42 / 0.15
Calculate: n = 280
Percent Change
change% = (new − old) / old × 100%
“Of” is always the original (base). A 25% increase then 20% decrease: multiply (1.25)(0.80), not add percents.
Example
Problem: Price $80 increases 15%, then the new price decreases 10%. Final price?
80 × 1.15 × 0.90
↓
= 92 × 0.90 = 82.8
Answer: $82.80
Mixtures (Weighted Average)
(m₁c₁ + m₂c₂) / (m₁ + m₂) = c_mix
m = amount (mass), c = concentration (fraction or %). Same idea: average speed = total distance ÷ total time (not average of speeds unless equal times).
Example
Problem: Mix 20 L of 30% acid with 10 L of 60% acid. What % acid is the mixture?
Acid = 20(0.30) + 10(0.60) = 6 + 6 = 12 L
↓
Total = 30 L → 12/30 = 0.40
Answer: 40% acid
Work Rates
Rate = 1 / time · 1/T = 1/A + 1/B + …
Each worker’s rate is “job per hour.” Working together, add rates. Opposite: one fills, one drains → subtract rates.
Example
Problem: A can finish a job in 6 h, B in 12 h. How long together?
1/T = 1/6 + 1/12 = 2/12 + 1/12 = 3/12
↓
1/T = 1/4 → T = 4
Answer: 4 hours together
How to Approach Problems

Rates & Word-Problem Framework

1
Label units: Write distance, rate, time (or work, workers, hours). Make sure rates and times are compatible (mph → hours, not minutes).
2
Ratios: Set equal fractions or use a proportion table. If a : b = a′ : b′, then a/b = a′/b′ (cross-multiply).
3
Percents: Convert “p%” to p/100 or 0.0p before algebra. Identify what “of” refers to — that’s the base.
4
Mixtures: Track the amount of pure substance (acid, salt, alcohol): “amount × concentration” = contribution, then divide by total amount.
5
Work: Use “1 job” as the whole. Add individual rates for cooperation; subtract if one process undoes the other.
Gotchas — Click to Expand
⚠️ Average Speed ≠ Average of Speeds +
If you go 30 mph for 2 h and 60 mph for 1 h, average speed is total distance ÷ total time = (60+60)/3 = 40 mph, not (30+60)/2. Only average the speeds when the times are equal.
⚠️ Percent Increase vs. “Percent Of” +
“Increase by 20%” means multiply by 1.20. “20% of the new value” is different from “20% of the original.” Read the base of the percent carefully.
⚠️ Work: Don’t Average Times +
Two people who take 6 h and 12 h alone do not take 9 h together. Add rates: 1/6 + 1/12 = 1/4 → 4 h together.
⚠️ Mixture vs. Replacement +
Mixing two solutions: use weighted average. Replace some volume with water: the amount of solute changes — subtract the fraction removed, then add new liquid if needed.
⚠️ Same Units +
Convert minutes to hours (or vice versa) before using d = rt. Mixing “miles per minute” with “hours” without converting is a common source of wrong answers by a factor of 60.
Practice Problems
EX 01 A train travels 120 miles at 40 mph, then 90 miles at 60 mph. What is its average speed for the entire trip? ▶
Solution
Time for first leg: 120/40 = 3 h. Second leg: 90/60 = 1.5 h.

Total distance = 120 + 90 = 210 mi. Total time = 3 + 1.5 = 4.5 h.

Average speed = 210 / 4.5 = 210 / (9/2) = 420/9 140/3 mph (≈ 46.67 mph)
EX 02 How many liters of pure water must be added to 40 L of 25% saline to make a 10% saline solution? ▶
Solution
Salt is fixed: 0.25 × 40 = 10 L salt.

Let x = liters of water added. New volume = (40 + x) L, still 10 L salt.
10 / (40 + x) = 0.10 → 10 = 0.10(40 + x) → 100 = 40 + x 60 L
EX 03 Pipe A fills a tank in 4 hours, pipe B drains it in 6 hours. When both are open, how long to fill the tank from empty? ▶
Solution
Rate of A: +1/4 tank per hour. Rate of B: −1/6 tank per hour.

Net rate: 1/4 − 1/6 = 3/12 − 2/12 = 1/12 tank per hour.

Time to fill 1 tank: T = 1 / (1/12) 12 hours

Things to Always Remember
🔢
0! = 1, not 0. This trips people up on C(n,0) and edge cases.
🎲
Probabilities must sum to 1. Always sanity-check your answer isn't > 1.
🔄
C(n,r) = C(n, n−r). Choosing 7 from 10 = choosing the 3 to leave out.
📐
PIE: alternate + for odd subsets, − for even subsets. Don't flip the signs.
🧲
"Glue" technique: treat a group as one unit, then multiply by the unit's internal arrangements.
🔁
Derangements: D(1)=0, D(2)=1, D(3)=2, D(4)=9, D(5)=44. Memorize D(2)–D(5).
⭕
Circular: fix one person, arrange the remaining (n−1) relative to them.
📊
Stars & Bars: works only for identical items. Distinct items in bins = kⁿ.
🧩
For casework, make sure your cases are mutually exclusive and exhaustive — no overlaps, no gaps.
🎯
Complement is your best friend at state level. If counting directly needs 3+ cases, try flipping it.
📌
n items, k chosen: combinations don't care about order. If selecting for different roles → permutation.
⚡
Geometric probability: reduce to 1D or 2D areas/lengths. Use symmetry whenever possible to simplify.
⏱️
Average speed: total distance ÷ total time. Successive % changes: multiply the factors (1 ± p/100).
🔧
Work problems: add rates (1/time). Opposing fill/drain → net rate can be positive or negative.
